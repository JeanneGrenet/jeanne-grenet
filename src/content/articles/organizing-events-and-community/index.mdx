I used to think that being a good developer meant getting better at code.  
Reading documentation, building side projects, watching conference talks at 1.5x speed.

And then I started actually going to the conferences.

Something shifted. Not in my technical skills, at least not directly, but in how I understood what this job is *really* about.  
Because tech isn't just a stack. It's people. And communities are where those people meet.


## 🌱 Where it started: a teenager in a Scratch workshop

I keep coming back to the same memory when I try to explain why I care about all of this.

I was a teenager, sitting in a room full of kids I didn't know.  
It was a **Devoxx4Kids** workshop. We were building something in Scratch, nothing complicated, but at some point it *worked*, and the room came alive a little.

That afternoon didn't teach me to code. What it did was show me that programming could feel like something.  
That it could be creative, collaborative, and genuinely joyful, not just a skill you acquire for a job.

> That single workshop quietly shaped the path that led me here.

Years later, when I joined [Fork it! Community](https://www.forkit.community) and got the chance to co-organize **Fork it! For Kids**, I understood why: I wanted to build that kind of room for someone else.


## 🧒 Fork it! For Kids: the organizer's side

Fork it! For Kids is a free event we run in Rouen for children aged 7 to 16, where they get to discover robotics, web development, game design, and programming through hands-on workshops led by volunteers.

Two editions so far. Around 20 kids the first time, nearly 40 the second.

I won't pretend organizing it is easy. There's the logistics, the volunteer coordination, the last-minute technical glitches, the planning spreadsheets that never quite survive contact with reality.  
But there's also this: during our second edition, several kids **skipped their snack break** because they didn't want to stop coding.

I think about that moment a lot. No one asked them to stay. They just wanted to finish what they'd started.

That's the thing about events done right: you create the conditions, and then people surprise you.


## 💬 What I didn't expect about being an organizer

Before I started organizing, I thought the hard part would be the technical setup.  
It's not. The hard part is **the people management**, not in a frustrating way, but in a deeply human one.

Coordinating a team of volunteers who are all giving their time freely means you can't manage them like a project. You have to inspire them, trust them, and leave room for their own judgment.  
Every workshop facilitator brings something you couldn't have planned for: their way of explaining things, their patience, their enthusiasm. And that's what makes it work.

I also didn't expect how much **I would get from it** as an attendee of my own event.  
Watching a 10-year-old figure out why their robot isn't following the line, and then fix it, teaches you something about how people learn that no book really captures.


## 🤝 Why community matters beyond the events

Fork it! For Kids is one piece of a much bigger thing for me.

I'm part of Fork it! Community more broadly: I attend meetups, I've given talks, I help with organization.  
But I'd say the same thing about any tech community, because the value isn't specific to one name or one structure.

**A tech community is where you remember you're not alone.**  
When you're deep in a problem at work, it's easy to feel like you're the only one who has ever struggled with this. A community is where you discover that someone else hit the exact same wall, found a way through, and is happy to tell you about it.

**It's where you grow in ways your job can't always offer.**  
Side projects, conference talks, workshops: these push you in directions that a sprint backlog rarely does. I've learned more about how to communicate technical ideas clearly from preparing a 20-minute talk than from years of pull request descriptions.

**And it's where you give back what was given to you.**  
That Devoxx4Kids workshop I attended as a teenager? Someone organized it. Someone wrote the curriculum, found the venue, showed up on a Saturday to make it happen.  
I didn't know it then, but that person was building the room I'd eventually want to build for someone else.

That's the loop I want to be part of.


## 🔭 What's next

I want to keep showing up. To meetups where I know nobody. To talks where I might disagree with the speaker. To events I help organize, or not, not because it's efficient, but because it's worth it.

Because I've learned that the best thing about being in a tech community isn't what you get out of it.  
It's what you find yourself wanting to give.
